       Introduction

    Air pollution can seriously affect the health of people. Even though air quality in Europe has improved over recent years, according to the European Environment Agency air pollution is the biggest environmental health risk in Europe, in particular in cities. In order to ensure effective transition to more live-able and breathable cities, the problem of air pollution requires coordinated actions from different angles. Besides long-term preventive action, for instance through the promotion of renewable energy, solutions are required to minimize exposure to pollution, such as establishing change in personal behaviour, for instance adapting transportation type or minimizing exposure to pollution. Smart city applications can help addressing the problem of minimizing exposure to pollution. Smart city applications are typically built using the Internet-of-Things (IoT). Engineering such IoT systems raises challenging problems as these systems typically consists of a infrastructure devices deployed over the city area combined with mobile devices used on the users side. To address this problem, we introduce HeyCitI, short for Healthy Cycling in a City using IoT, a smart city solution that helps cyclists to determine a healthy path through a city area. HeyCitI finds the most healthy path between a start and end location and dynamically adapts the path on the way when using data from sensors deployed in the environment. To investigate, design, implement, and test HeyCitI before actual deployment in the field, engineers can make use of simulation. The requirements for the simulator are fourfold: (i) fine grained environment modeling, including pollution and sensors, (ii) handle a representative number of users (cyclists), (iii) plug and play support for dynamic adaptation of paths, (iv) direct mapping to a real IoT network. This webpage describes the HeyCitI simulator and provides links with resources that can be downloaded for experimentation.

      We have implemented the HeyCitI simulator in Java to allow researchers to evaluate and compare pathfinding solutions and analyze cyclists behaviour in different environments. The simulator comes packed with a few setups for the simulator for Leuven.
